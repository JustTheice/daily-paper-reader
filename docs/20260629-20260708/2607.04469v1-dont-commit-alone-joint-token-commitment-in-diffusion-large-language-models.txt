# Abstract 

Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) commit multiple tokens per denoising step by decoding each selected position independently from the shared context; when those positions are dependent, the resulting factorization error is captured by conditional total correlation, which confidence-based selection cannot observe from marginals alone. We propose C OCOMMIT , a marker-gated coordination pass that briefly defers commitment: after the usual bundle selection, a learned marker announces the commit set and the backbone’s last-n layers are re-applied so marked positions coordinate—approximating joint-mode decoding—before greedy argmax writes tokens. The method reuses existing weights with one extra partial forward pass and no auxiliary model. On LLaDA2.1-mini with LoRA adapters and matched greedy inference, joint commitment improves accuracy on all six benchmarks we evaluate, with the largest gains on reasoning and exact-answer tasks. 

# 1 Introduction 

Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) generate text by iteratively revealing tokens from a masked sequence, committing multiple positions in parallel at each denoising step ( 2, 22 , 24 , 27 , 35 ). Parallelism is the source of their speed advantage over autoregressive decoding, and also the source of a characteristic error: tokens committed in the same step are predicted from the same context but not from each other, so each is drawn from its own marginal distribution while the tuple they form may belong to no coherent joint completion ( 17 ). This failure is usually described operationally—“parallel tokens can be mutually inconsistent”—and treated downstream, by editing or remasking committed tokens after the fact ( 5, 30 , 33 ). We instead start from what the error is . For a commit set S and context ctx , factorized commitment replaces the joint posterior 

p(xS | ctx) with the product of marginals Q 

> i∈S

p(xi | ctx) . The gap between the two is the conditional total correlation TC( xS | ctx) , and it vanishes if and only if the committed positions are conditionally independent given the context. The entire quality–speed trade-off of parallel decoding is governed by this single quantity: autoregressive decoding pays for exactness with sequentiality ( |S| = 1 , TC ≡ 0); parallel commitment in a dLLM pays TC( xS | ctx) per step for speed. This view exposes a structural blind spot in how current samplers choose which positions to commit. Confidence-based selection—committing the positions whose marginals are most peaked—measures marginal entropy, not dependence. Marginals are averages over the joint modes of the posterior: in a context compatible with both “New York City” and “San Francisco”, the first position assigns high probability to both New 

and San , and each position’s confidence can be inherited from a different mode. High marginal confidence therefore does not imply low total correlation, and no selection rule that reads only marginals can bound the commitment error. The failure is not that confidence heuristics are poorly tuned; it is that the information they would need is absent from the quantities they observe. If selection cannot avoid dependent bundles, commitment must handle them. Our key observation is that the information required for coordination is already computed—the backbone’s hidden states encode the joint 1

structure of the block—but the output interface discards it: a factorized readout head projects each position onto the vocabulary independently, at exactly the moment the positions’ fates are being decided. What is missing is not capacity but a communication channel at decision time .COCOMMIT adds that channel with minimal machinery (Figure 1). Each denoising step is split into two stages that share one prefix computation. Stage A runs the last n transformer layers without modification and selects the commit bundle by the standard confidence rule; nothing is written. A learned marker vector is then added to the hidden states of the selected positions, announcing “these positions are being decided now”, and Stage B re-applies the same last-n layers. Attention among the marked positions lets the bundle coordinate— break the mode averaging—before any token is committed; the final tokens are read from the Stage B logits. Under greedy commitment (the standard regime for these models), this deterministic coordination followed by per-position argmax yields a tuple from a single joint mode rather than a mixture of modes: we target the joint mode of p(xS | ctx) , not samples from the joint. Although the readout remains one softmax per position, coordination fixes which mode the bundle resolves; the claim is scoped to greedy argmax—under temperature sampling, per-position noise can still split modes, and a wrong but self-consistent mode may be committed confidently, errors that orthogonal repair methods address after the fact. In training, branch 2 predicts marked positions given the marker announcement; edit/clean terms hold unmarked predictions fixed, so the announcement helps only through attention among marked positions, and the adapters learn to make the bundle consult itself. The mechanism generalizes along two orthogonal axes. First, the decision set need not be restricted to masked positions: marking a visible token would submit its current value for joint re-negotiation, unifying commitment (mask-to-token) and revision (token-to-token) as one operation; our experiments mark masked commit bundles only. Second, the coordination pass may be iterated : K=0 recovers factorized decoding, 

K=1 is our main setting, and deeper iteration would trade test-time compute for stronger within-bundle coordination—in the limit, with injected noise and denoising training, toward a conditional joint model over the bundle in the spirit of MAR (20). dLLMs fail along two orthogonal axes: within-step factorization—incoherent tuples assembled from confident factorized marginals, with error TC( xS | ctx) —and across-step semantic error, where an early wrong commit steers later predictions as confident context. C OCOMMIT targets the first at the instant of commitment; repair methods target the second; the two are complementary rather than alternative fixes. This paper makes the following contributions: • We motivate joint token commitment by framing within-step parallel error as conditional total correlation of the commit bundle, and by identifying a blind spot in confidence-based selection: per-position marginals average over joint modes and carry no dependency signal. • We propose C OCOMMIT , a marker-gated coordination pass that reuses the backbone’s last-n layers so the commit bundle exchanges information before tokens are written—no new head, no external dependency model, one extra partial forward pass (Section 4). • We evaluate C OCOMMIT on six standard benchmarks under a matched inference protocol, training only LoRA adapters and marker vectors on generic prose; joint commitment improves all six tasks, with the largest gains on reasoning and exact-answer benchmarks while remaining positive elsewhere (Table 1). 

# 2 Related Work 

Discrete diffusion language models. D3PM ( 2) established diffusion over discrete tokens and identified absorbing-state (masking) corruption as best suited to text; SEDD ( 22 ), MDLM ( 27 ), and MD4 ( 29 ) progres-sively simplified the formulation, and RADD ( 26 ) showed that the learned predictor is exactly a conditional distribution over clean tokens given the unmasked context. LLaDA ( 24 ) and its successors ( 5, 25 ) scaled the recipe to large models, Dream ( 35 ) and DiffuLLaMA ( 10 ) adapted autoregressive checkpoints, and BD3-LM ( 1 ) introduced the semi-autoregressive block schedule that LLaDA2.1 ( 5) adopts. All of these commit multiple positions per step by independent argmax or sampling from per-position distributions; our work targets precisely this commitment rule and leaves the rest of the pipeline unchanged. 2Figure 1: Factorized versus joint commitment (one denoising step). Continuous diffusion language models. A parallel line keeps the generative trajectory in a continuous space and discretizes late. ELF ( 14 ) denoises in a frozen encoder’s embedding space with flow matching and maps to tokens only at the terminal step; Cola ( 12 ) transports a latent prior with a block-causal DiT and decodes text from the latent. In our vocabulary, these models coordinate positions continuously throughout the trajectory and never perform a mid-trajectory parallel factorized commit; the price is the loss of early discrete pruning and a terminal continuous-to-discrete mapping step. We treat this family as the late-discretization reference point of the design space, not as a competitor: C OCOMMIT localizes ELF-style pre-discretization coordination to each commit instant of an early-committing model, rather than returning to fully late discretization. 

Dependency-aware parallel decoding. The inconsistency of independently decoded parallel tokens predates dLLMs: non-autoregressive translation observed it as the multimodality problem ( 11 ), and iterative refinement schemes such as Mask-Predict ( 9 ) amortize it across steps. ParallelBench ( 17 ) documents the same failure in modern dLLMs. Closest to our diagnosis, discrete copula diffusion ( 21 ) shows that per-position marginals miss the dependency structure (the copula) of the joint and restores it with a separate autoregressive model at inference. We internalize the correction instead: the backbone itself is made dependency-aware at commit time through a marker-gated re-application of its own last-n layers, requiring no auxiliary model and matching the greedy-commitment regime these systems actually use. 

Repair-based self-correction. An orthogonal family repairs commitments after they are written: token-editing phases in LLaDA2.1 ( 5), uniform or targeted remasking ( 30 , 33 , 36 ), and training-based correctors ( 13 ,15 , 18 , 28 , 34 ). These methods act on the across-step axis—undoing or overwriting tokens whose semantics turn out to be wrong—whereas C OCOMMIT acts within the step, reducing the factorization error at the source so that fewer inconsistent tuples are written at all. 3Joint prediction and iterated computation. MAR ( 20 ) models a block’s joint distribution with a diffusion head on top of a transformer backbone; our Stage B can be seen as the cheapest member of that family—a single deterministic coordination pass with shared weights and cross-entropy readout, applied online to exactly the positions about to be committed rather than offline to a whole block. Iterating the coordination pass connects to weight-tied recurrent computation ( 3, 6 ) and to recurrent-depth test-time scaling ( 8 ): K buys coordination depth at commit time, a new axis of test-time compute for parallel decoders. Classical iterated conditional modes ( 4 ) is the token-feedback analogue of iterating coordination on hidden states rather than discrete tokens. 

# 3 Preliminaries: LLaDA2.1 Training and Inference 

We use LLaDA2.1 ( 5 ) as the base system, a semi-autoregressive block-diffusion dLLM that generates text block by block, using iterative denoising within each block. This section reviews the base training and inference procedure used by LLaDA2.1. 

# 3.1 Base Training 

Block-diffusion forward. The sequence is partitioned into blocks of B tokens. Attention is block-causal: within a block, every position—including [M] positions—attends to every other position bidirectionally ;across blocks, attention is causal, so block j sees only blocks 0, . . . , j . Training and inference share this structure. 

M2T/T2T supervision. Training corrupts a clean sequence x = ( x1, . . . , x T ) into an input z containing three kinds of positions: a masked set M (input [M] ), a wrong-visible set W (visible tokens differing from the clean token), and a clean-visible set G (visible tokens equal to the clean token). The model reads the full corrupted input z and outputs one token distribution pi per position; every supervised position is trained with cross-entropy toward its clean token:

# 3.2 Base Inference 

Block-wise denoising. The response is generated block by block, left to right. Prompt tokens enter the sequence unmasked and are never edited. Once a response block is complete, its tokens are never edited again; only positions in the current block are initialized as [M] and updated by denoising. 

Factorized M2T/T2T updates. The current block is decoded by iterative denoising. Let ctx denote the fixed context outside the current block—the prompt and completed response blocks, whose tokens are never edited again. Let z denote the current state of the block, containing already-committed tokens and remaining [M] 

positions. Each step runs one forward pass through the L-layer backbone on (ctx , z ), and the language-model head reads out one independent distribution pθ (· | ctx , z ) per position in the current block. The T2T edits in the same step are also read from the same pre-update state z: they can revise already-visible tokens, but they do not condition on the newly written M2T tokens from that step. Thus a single step has no explicit joint choice among simultaneous M2T writes, or between those writes and simultaneous T2T edits. We refer to this update rule as factorized commitment .

# 4 Method 

LLaDA2.1’s original decoding step is factorized. C OCOMMIT replaces the original M2T/T2T update with a marker-gated coordination pass, turning the step’s updates into a joint-mode commitment over the selected commit bundle. Training (Section 4.1) teaches the backbone this behavior through two branches; inference (Section 4.2) runs the same two branches serially (Figure 2). 

Compute-constrained validation. The principled way to teach marker-gated coordination would integrate it throughout pretraining, instruction tuning, and alignment so the backbone learns to coordinate at every denoising stage. Such full-scale training would be the cleanest validation of our account, but it is beyond our available compute. We therefore use a deliberately minimal implementation: starting from the released LLaDA2.1-mini checkpoint, we freeze the backbone and add only low-rank LoRA adapters together with the marker vectors, trained in a short continued-pretraining pass on generic English prose (Section 5.1). This setting is not intended as the final recipe; it is a stress test of the mechanism—if within-step coordination matters, the signal should be visible even when only a small adapter teaches the split forward on top of an already-finished model with out-of-domain CPT text. The empirical results should therefore be read as evidence that marker-gated coordination can be learned at commitment time, not as a claim that this LoRA checkpoint is the best possible instantiation.

Corrupted inputs z are built from clean x following Yao (34) , with the marker branch of Section 4.1 added on top; branch 2 marks a random bundle of s positions with s ∼ Uniform {1, . . . , 16 }. We optimize with AdamW at learning rate 5×10 −6 for one epoch (global batch size 8, sequence length 2048, cosine schedule with 1% warmup) under FSDP2 with bf16 and gradient checkpointing, using loss weights (λm2t , λ edit , λ clean ) = (1 .0, 0.3, 0.2) ,and evaluate the checkpoint at training step 6250. 

Benchmarks. The reported set covers six benchmarks grouped into three categories. TriviaQA is an open-domain factual QA benchmark whose validation examples are scored by exact match against answer aliases ( 16 ). MMLU-Pro is a multiple-choice knowledge benchmark scored by accuracy ( 31 ). DROP is a reading-comprehension benchmark requiring discrete reasoning over paragraphs, scored by exact match on the validation split ( 7). CMATH is a Chinese elementary-school math benchmark with short numeric final answers, scored by numeric exact match ( 32 ). AIME 2025 is a competition-math benchmark with integer answers; we report pass@1 on the 30-problem 2025 set ( 23 ). GSM Plus is a grade-school math benchmark with numeric final answers, scored by accuracy (19).

# 6 Conclusion 

Diffusion large language models pay for speed with a per-step distributional error that has a name: the conditional total correlation of the commit bundle. Confidence-based selection has no view of this error: per-position marginals average over joint modes and carry no dependency signal. C OCOMMIT responds at the only place the error can be intercepted—the instant of commitment—by announcing the bundle with a learned marker and re-applying the backbone’s own last-n layers so the bundle coordinates before tokens are written. Under greedy commitment, one deterministic coordination round is designed to replace independent marginal argmax with an approximate joint-mode argmax, at the cost of a single extra partial forward pass, lightweight adapters, and one marker vector. The mechanism suggests two extensions we do not evaluate here: marking visible tokens would unify commitment with revision, and iterating the coordination pass would trade test-time compute for stronger jointness, spanning factorized decoding to conditional diffusion over the bundle. Within-step coordination does not remove the need for across-step repair—a coordinated bundle can still be wrong—but it targets the structural error that repair would otherwise be left to clean up. 

Limitations. COCOMMIT requires fine-tuning (adapters and the marker); it is not a training-free rule. The coordination claim is scoped to greedy commitment: under temperature sampling, per-position noise can still split a bundle across modes unless the coordinated marginals are sharp. The hidden-state iteration for 

K > 1 carries no theoretical monotonicity guarantee, and our checkpoints are trained at K = 1 , so deeper coordination requires training with sampled K. Marking visible tokens for joint revision is discussed but not evaluated here. Finally, all experiments use one model family (LLaDA2.1-mini); the generality of the mode-averaging diagnosis across dLLM families remains to be established.
